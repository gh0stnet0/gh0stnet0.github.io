OpSec layers
why your threat model is probably wrong

OpSec isn't a checklist. It's a continuous process of identifying what you're protecting, from whom, and whether your current measures actually match that reality.

the model most people use

Most people's threat model is implicit. They use a VPN because someone told them to. They use Signal because it sounds secure. They clear their browser history because it feels private. None of this is a threat model. It's security theater.

A real threat model starts with a question: who specifically might want your data, what specifically would they do with it, and what capabilities do they actually have? The answer defines everything else.

build your threat model first

adversary capabilities your exposure priority
Script kiddie Public exploits, port scanners, credential stuffing Exposed services, reused passwords low
Targeted attacker OSINT, phishing, custom tooling, persistence Public footprint, email, social engineering vectors medium
ISP / network observer Full traffic visibility, metadata, timing analysis Unencrypted traffic, DNS queries, connection patterns medium
Nation state / LEA Legal compulsion, hardware implants, zero-days, SIGINT Everything if they decide you're a target high
common mistake: building nation-state-level OpSec against script kiddies. overkill creates friction without proportional protection. match your measures to your actual adversary.

layer 01 — network

Your IP is your identity on the public internet. Everything flows from there.

  • DNS over HTTPS or DNS over TLS — stop leaking queries to your ISP
  • VPN or Tor depending on threat model — not both blindly
  • Firewall with explicit allow rules, default deny
  • Network segmentation — lab traffic never touches personal traffic
# DNS leak check
dig +short myip.opendns.com @resolver1.opendns.com
curl -s https://dns.google/resolve?name=whoami.akamai.net&type=A
 
# verify no DNS leaks through tunnel
cat /etc/resolv.conf
resolvectl status | grep "DNS Server"

layer 02 — identity separation

Compartmentalization means different identities for different contexts. Not aliases — completely separate operational identities with no crossover in behavior, infrastructure, or metadata.

  • Separate devices or VMs per identity context
  • Different network paths per context — never mix
  • No behavioral crossover — writing style, timing, habits
  • No shared infrastructure — same VPS for two identities breaks both
the haircut problem: you can do everything right technically and still be identified by when you're active, how you write, or that you always make mistakes after midnight. metadata and behavior are harder to sanitize than packets.

layer 03 — operational discipline

Most OpSec failures aren't technical. They're behavioral. The best encryption doesn't protect you from talking about an operation on an unsecured channel.

  • Need-to-know — information shared is information that can leak
  • Consistent procedures — habits are predictable, predictability is exposure
  • Minimize footprint — accounts, services and data you don't need are attack surface
  • Assume logging — act as if every action is being recorded somewhere

the check you should run regularly

# what's listening on your machine right now
ss -tlnp
 
# outbound connections — who are you talking to
ss -tnp state established
 
# processes with network access
lsof -i -n -P | grep ESTABLISHED
 
# check for unexpected cron jobs
crontab -l; cat /etc/cron* 2>/dev/null

If something in that output surprises you, your OpSec has already failed somewhere. Surprises are indicators. Indicators require investigation.
